# Meta Area: The Human as a Conditioned Decision Space

In Offensive Security, the human being is not regarded as a "source of error," but as a dynamic decision system whose behavior is highly dependent on the context in which decisions are made. The psychological attack vector does not originate from the individual traits of a target person, but from the way the decision environment is designed.

The hacker operates on a meta-level:
He does not directly target beliefs, emotions, or personality, but alters the parameters under which cognitive processes take place. These parameters determine whether reflective, controlled cognition or simplified, heuristic decision mechanisms dominate.

The central assumption is this:
Humans do not make consistently rational decisions — they make contextually efficient ones.
Efficiency here does not mean optimality but the reduction of cognitive cost. The human brain is built to conserve energy. Whenever the situation allows or demands it, analytical depth is sacrificed in favor of quick, sufficiently plausible decisions.

The psychological attack surface emerges precisely at this transition.

### Decision as Process, Not as Trait
A common misconception in security contexts is to attribute poor decisions to individual weaknesses ("careless," "gullible," "incompetent"). This view is analytically useless.
What matters is not *who* decides, but *under which conditions* decisions are made.

Decisions can be modeled as the result of:
- Available time 
- Perceived consequences 
- Cognitive load 
- Emotional activation level 
- Social context 

These parameters are externally influenceable.

The hacker leverages this by constructing situations where:
- Analysis becomes unattractive or impossible 
- Simplification appears functional 
- Shortcuts are perceived as “reasonable” 

Thus, the decision shifts not in content but in structure.

### The True Attack Vector: Context Control
Psychological Offensive Security does not operate on the level of arguments, but on the level of environmental framing.

The attacker does not ask: 
*“How do I convince this person?”* 
but rather: 
*“What must the situation look like so that this person convinces themselves?”*

The target system performs the actual error autonomously. 
The attacker only provides the conditions.

This distinction is crucial because it explains why the same person can behave completely differently in varying contexts — without any change in personality or intelligence.

### Contrast with Classical Social Engineering
Traditional social engineering often focuses on:
- Deception 
- Lying 
- Role imitation 

The approach described here is more fundamental. 
Deception is optional. 
Manipulation occurs primarily through cognitive environment design, not through false content.

This makes the approach:
- Harder to detect 
- More resilient against awareness training 
- More reproducible across different targets 

### Transition Point (No Taxonomy Yet)
Only after defining this meta-space does classification become meaningful. 
Cognitive biases are not causes but effects that reliably emerge under certain conditions. 
The taxonomy describes what becomes *visible*, not what is *manipulated*. 
What is manipulated is the context. 
What becomes visible are the distortions.
